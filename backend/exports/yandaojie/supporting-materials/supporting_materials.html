1. Purpose & how to read this dossier

This pack strengthens the evidence base of our submission. GlowingStar Yandaojie (“研道阶”, literally a staircase for inquiring into knowledge) is an AI-powered knowledge-defense tool for primary-school pupils. Rather than grading multiple-choice answers, it runs a short, oral-style defense—much like a thesis viva—in which a teacher-modelled AI examiner reads a child’s reflection, then asks one probing open question at a time, adapting each follow-up to the pupil’s previous answer and diagnosing exactly which learning objectives are mastered and which are not.

The materials below map onto the categories the competition invites—evaluation reports, implementation frameworks, policy/technical documents, testimonials, research references and classroom documentation—and are designed to demonstrate impact, sustainability and institutional embedding. Quantitative claims are drawn directly from an anonymised export of live pilot data (see §6 and §10).

3. Implementation framework — the pedagogical model

Each defense follows a fixed, teacher-designed structure so that the experience is consistent, fair and tightly aligned to the curriculum’s stated learning objectives.

3.1 The learner journey

  1. Reflect. The pupil first writes a brief reflection against each lesson objective: what I learned and what I’m still unsure about. This primes metacognition and gives the examiner a starting point.
  2. Defend (5 rounds). An AI examiner—modelled on “a rigorous but friendly Grade-6 teacher”—asks exactly one open question per round, for up to five rounds. Every question must explicitly build on something the child just said, target a named objective, and probe for weak reasoning, missing evidence or over-generalisation rather than reward recall.
  3. Diagnose. After every round the system produces a structured diagnosis: which knowledge points the pupil has mastered and which are not yet mastered, with a one-line rationale each. This is the formative payload teachers act on.

3.2 Design guarantees (enforced in the examiner)

  • Open questions only (“why / how / explain”)—no yes/no or multiple-choice prompts.
  • One question, one question-mark per turn; no “and / also” compound questions.
  • Questions are short enough for a Grade-6 pupil to answer in ~30 seconds.
  • The examiner never answers for the pupil and never repeats an earlier question.
  • Every question is tied to a specific curriculum objective; off-topic drift is disallowed.
  • Subject-aware behaviour (e.g. for English, the examiner states explicitly when an answer must be given in English).
Why “defense” instead of a quiz

A quiz measures whether a final answer is right. A defense surfaces how a child reasons—and where the reasoning breaks. Because each follow-up is generated from the pupil’s own words, the tool adapts to 50 different children without 50 hand-written question banks, and it hands the teacher an objective-level mastery map for the whole class.

5. Classroom documentation — annotated transcripts

Verbatim, anonymised dialogues from the pilot. They show the examiner building each question on the pupil’s previous words and steering toward the objective. Original Chinese is preserved with English glosses.

Science · objective: classify physical vs. chemical change by “is a new substance formed?”

Q1 (AI): 你提到厨房里物质很多都在变化，请举一个具体的例子，并说说变化后是否产生了新物质？
“You said many things in the kitchen change—give one concrete example and say whether a new substance forms.”

A (pupil): 水变成了好喝的汤 — “Water turned into tasty soup.”

Q2 (AI): 你说水变成了好喝的汤，那汤里的水还是原来的水吗？
Builds directly on the child’s answer to test the core concept.

A: 不是原来的了 因为有别的物质

Q5 (AI): 水在加热时会变成水蒸气，水蒸气是水产生的新物质吗？

A: 不是 状态不一样 — “No—it’s a different state.” (correct distinction)

Mathematics · objective: meaning of a coordinate pair and scaling on grid paper

Q (AI): 如果你在方格纸上看到一个点，写出的数对是（5，4），那么“5”这个数表示什么意思呢？

A: 我不知道 — gap surfaced; flagged “not mastered”.

Q (AI, later round): 既然数对（3，2）中的3是列、2是行，如果把两个数都乘2变成（6，4），这个点会向什么方向移动？
Escalates from naming to reasoning about scaling—exactly the lesson objective.

6. Technology, methodology & data provenance

6.1 Architecture

  • Frontend: Next.js 14 + Tailwind CSS pupil interface.
  • Backend: FastAPI service with typed schemas; a defense-orchestration service builds each prompt from objectives, reflections and prior turns.
  • Reasoning model: a large language model is prompted to return strict JSON containing the next question, the objective(s) it targets (with rationale) and the mastery diagnosis—making every AI decision auditable.
  • Persistence: sessions, questions, turns, events and errors are stored for analysis.

6.2 Reproducible evaluation pipeline

A documented export script extracts every collection to (a) full-fidelity JSONL archives and (b) flattened CSV tables for Excel / R / SPSS / pandas, plus a manifest.json with row counts. Real pupils are separated from QA accounts by a deterministic rule, so the impact figures in §4 are reproducible from the raw archive.

7. Sustainability & institutional embedding

  • Curriculum-anchored, not bolt-on. Defenses are authored against the exact objectives of standard Grade-6 lessons (Maths, Science, English), so the tool slots into existing teaching rather than competing with it.
  • Teacher in the loop. The output is a class-level mastery map teachers use to plan re-teaching—the human teacher remains the decision-maker.
  • Maintainable engineering. A single monorepo with isolated frontend/backend workspaces, linting and type-checking supports independent, low-risk iteration.
  • Reliability instrumentation. Errors and events are logged and exported, giving an honest operational picture and a basis for continuous improvement.
  • Cost scalability. One adaptive examiner replaces bespoke per-pupil question banks, so coverage scales to more pupils and subjects without proportional teacher workload.
  • Roadmap: add scaffolding for low-completion topics (see Maths in §4.2), expand subject coverage, and longitudinal tracking of mastery gains across terms.

9. Research references

The design draws on established learning science:

  1. Black, P. & Wiliam, D. (1998). Assessment and Classroom Learning. Assessment in Education, 5(1), 7–74. — foundational case for formative assessment and feedback.
  2. Vygotsky, L. S. (1978). Mind in Society. — the Zone of Proximal Development; questioning that adapts to the learner.
  3. Chi, M. T. H. et al. (1994). Eliciting self-explanations improves understanding. Cognitive Science, 18(3). — the “explain why” effect the defense exploits.
  4. Roediger, H. L. & Karpicke, J. D. (2006). The power of testing/retrieval practice. Perspectives on Psychological Science, 1(3).
  5. Graesser, A. C. et al. (2005). AutoTutor and dialogue-based intelligent tutoring. IEEE Trans. Education. — precedent for conversational, Socratic AI tutoring.
  6. OECD (2005). Formative Assessment: Improving Learning in Secondary Classrooms. — policy framing for embedding formative practice.
